# DORA Key Pillars and Compliance Obligations — EU Bank

## Compliance Deadline

**DORA (Regulation (EU) 2022/2554) became applicable on 17 January 2025** (Art. 64). As an EU credit institution, you are already subject to DORA's full requirements. If you have not yet completed your compliance programme, you are operating in breach of a directly applicable EU regulation.

---

## DORA Structure: Five Operational Pillars

DORA is structured across 9 Chapters. For an EU bank, the five core compliance pillars map to Chapters II–VI:

---

### Pillar 1 — ICT Risk Management Framework (Chapter II, Art. 5–16)

This is the foundational, proactive governance obligation.

**Art. 5 — Board Accountability:**
- The management body (board) bears ultimate responsibility for ICT risk (Art. 5(1))
- The board must define ICT risk appetite and strategy (Art. 5(2)(a))
- The board must formally approve ICT security policies (Art. 5(2)(b))
- The board must ensure adequate ICT budgets and training (Art. 5(2)(d)–(e))

**Art. 6 — ICT Risk Management Framework (RMF):**
- Maintain a comprehensive, documented ICT RMF covering strategies, policies, procedures, protocols and tools (Art. 6(1)–(2))
- Review the RMF after major incidents and at least annually (Art. 6(5))
- Key RTS: CDR (EU) 2024/1774

**Art. 8 — Identification:**
- Identify and classify all ICT assets supporting critical or important functions
- Maintain an ICT asset register with dependency mapping (Art. 8(4))

**Art. 9 — Protection:**
- Implement physical and logical access controls, network segmentation, encryption, and patch management (Art. 9(2)–(4))

**Art. 10 — Detection:**
- Deploy monitoring tools with defined alerting thresholds for anomalous activities (Art. 10(1))

**Art. 11 — Response and Recovery:**
- Documented ICT business continuity policy with BIA, RTO/RPO targets, and annual testing (Art. 11(1)–(6))

**Art. 12 — Backup:**
- Implement backup policies with defined scope, frequency, and offsite/separate storage; test restorability (Art. 12(1)–(3))

**Art. 13 — Learning:**
- Post-incident reviews, threat intelligence monitoring, and ICT security training programmes (Art. 13(1)–(6))

---

### Pillar 2 — ICT-Related Incident Management, Classification and Reporting (Chapter III, Art. 17–23)

This is the reactive, event-driven obligation — distinct from Pillar 1.

**Art. 17 — Incident Management Process:**
- Documented incident management process with defined classification thresholds and escalation paths

**Art. 18 — Classification:**
Incidents are classified using six criteria (Art. 18(1)):
- (a) Number of clients/counterparts affected and transaction value
- (b) Reputational impact
- (c) Duration and geographic spread
- (d) Data losses (availability, authenticity, integrity, confidentiality)
- (e) Criticality of affected services
- (f) Economic impact

Materiality thresholds are set in CDR (EU) 2024/1772.

**Art. 19 — Three-Stage Reporting to Competent Authority:**

| Stage | Deadline | Content |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Initial notification | 4 hours after classification as major | Basic facts, initial impact assessment |
| Intermediate report | 72 hours after classification as major | Updated assessment, root cause indications |
| Final report | 1 month after initial notification | Root cause analysis, lessons learned, recovery measures |

Key RTS: CDR (EU) 2025/301 | Key ITS: CIR (EU) 2025/302 (standard forms)

---

### Pillar 3 — Digital Operational Resilience Testing (Chapter IV, Art. 24–27)

**Art. 24 — Basic Testing Programme:**
- All financial entities must conduct an annual digital operational resilience testing programme
- Covers vulnerability assessments, gap analyses, network security assessments
- Must be performed by independent internal or external parties (Art. 24(4))

**Art. 25 — Testing Types:**
Vulnerability scans, source code reviews (where applicable), scenario-based tests, performance tests, and end-to-end tests.

**Art. 26 — TLPT (Threat-Led Penetration Testing):**
- Required for significant entities meeting criteria in Art. 26(8) (size, complexity, systemic relevance)
- Frequency: at least once every 3 years
- Must cover live production systems with intelligence-led red team scenarios
- Key RTS: CDR (EU) 2025/1190

---

### Pillar 4 — ICT Third-Party Risk Management (Chapter V, Art. 28–44)

**Art. 28 — Third-Party Risk Policy:**
- Adopt and maintain an ICT third-party risk policy (Art. 28(1))
- Maintain a Register of Information on all ICT service arrangements (Art. 28(3)) per CIR (EU) 2024/2956
- Assess ICT concentration risk (Art. 28(6))
- Develop exit strategies for critical arrangements (Art. 28(7))

**Art. 30 — Contractual Provisions:**
All contracts with ICT TPSPs supporting critical or important functions must include provisions covering:
- Service description, data locations, data protection, availability/security, audit rights, termination rights, data portability, and sub-contracting consent (Art. 30(2)(a)–(i))
- Key RTS: CDR (EU) 2024/1773

**Art. 31–44 — Critical TPSP Oversight:**
ESAs may designate ICT providers as Critical Third-Party Service Providers (CTPPs) subject to EU-level oversight by a Lead Overseer (EBA, ESMA, or EIOPA).

---

### Pillar 5 — Information Sharing (Chapter VI, Art. 45)

Financial entities may voluntarily participate in cyber threat intelligence sharing arrangements with other financial entities, subject to data protection and competition rules.

## Important Note on Proportionality

As a credit institution (bank), you are subject to the **full DORA framework** under Art. 6–14. The simplified ICT risk management framework (Art. 16) does not apply to banks — it is reserved for micro-enterprises and certain small, non-interconnected entities as defined in CDR (EU) 2024/1774.
