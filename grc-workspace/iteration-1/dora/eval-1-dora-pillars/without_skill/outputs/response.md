# DORA Key Pillars and Compliance Obligations — EU Bank

## Compliance Deadline

DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act), formally Regulation (EU) 2022/2554, entered into force in January 2023 and became applicable in **January 2025**. As an EU bank, you should already be operating under DORA's requirements.

---

## What Is DORA?

DORA is an EU regulation that creates a unified framework for digital operational resilience across the EU financial sector. It replaces and harmonises the patchwork of national and sector-specific ICT risk requirements that previously applied to banks and other financial entities.

DORA applies to a wide range of financial entities including banks (credit institutions), investment firms, insurance companies, payment institutions, crypto-asset service providers, and others.

---

## Key Pillars of DORA

### Pillar 1 — ICT Risk Management

DORA requires financial entities to establish and maintain a comprehensive ICT risk management framework. This includes:

- **Board-level governance**: Senior management and the board must be accountable for ICT risk. They must define risk appetite, approve ICT security policies, and allocate appropriate resources.
- **ICT asset identification**: Entities must identify and classify all ICT systems and assets, particularly those supporting critical or important business functions.
- **Protection measures**: Access controls, network security, encryption, patch management, and change management procedures.
- **Detection capabilities**: Monitoring tools and processes to detect anomalous activities and potential incidents.
- **Response and recovery**: Business continuity plans, disaster recovery procedures, with defined recovery time and recovery point objectives (RTO/RPO).
- **Backup policies**: Regular backups with tested restoration procedures.
- **Learning**: Post-incident reviews and ongoing threat intelligence.

### Pillar 2 — ICT Incident Management and Reporting

DORA establishes a harmonised approach to ICT incident management:

- **Incident classification**: Entities must classify ICT incidents based on criteria including the number of clients affected, reputational impact, duration, data losses, criticality of services, and economic impact.
- **Reporting obligations**: Major ICT incidents must be reported to competent authorities in multiple stages. Generally, there is an initial notification requirement (thought to be within a few hours), followed by intermediate and final reports over subsequent weeks.
- **Cyber threat reporting**: Entities may voluntarily report significant cyber threats.

### Pillar 3 — Digital Operational Resilience Testing

DORA introduces mandatory testing requirements:

- **Basic testing**: All financial entities must conduct regular resilience testing including vulnerability assessments, network security assessments, and scenario-based tests. These should occur at least annually for critical systems.
- **Advanced testing (TLPT)**: Larger, more significant financial entities must conduct Threat-Led Penetration Testing (TLPT) — an intelligence-led, red-team style exercise performed on live production systems. This is required periodically (likely every 2–3 years) by entities meeting certain size and complexity thresholds.

### Pillar 4 — ICT Third-Party Risk Management

Given the financial sector's heavy reliance on cloud providers and other technology vendors, DORA imposes extensive third-party risk requirements:

- **Third-party risk policy**: A formal policy for managing ICT service provider relationships.
- **Register of Information**: A comprehensive inventory of all ICT service arrangements.
- **Contractual requirements**: Contracts with providers of critical or important services must include specific provisions covering service descriptions, security standards, audit rights, exit rights, sub-contracting arrangements, and data portability.
- **Concentration risk**: Entities must assess and manage the risk of over-reliance on single providers.
- **Critical TPSP oversight**: The EU supervisory authorities (EBA, ESMA, EIOPA) can designate systemically important ICT providers as "critical" and subject them to direct EU-level oversight.

### Pillar 5 — Information Sharing

DORA encourages (but does not mandate) financial entities to participate in voluntary cyber threat intelligence sharing arrangements with other financial sector participants.

---

## Main Compliance Obligations for an EU Bank

As a credit institution, your key obligations include:

1. **Governance**: Board formally accountable for ICT risk; ICT risk appetite statement approved at board level
2. **ICT Risk Framework**: Documented, comprehensive framework reviewed at least annually
3. **Asset Management**: Maintained ICT asset register mapped to critical business functions
4. **Incident Process**: Formal classification and multi-stage reporting process in place
5. **Resilience Testing**: Annual basic testing programme; assess whether TLPT applies
6. **Third-Party Management**: Register of Information maintained; contracts reviewed for DORA compliance
7. **Training**: Regular ICT security and resilience training for staff

---

## Note on Proportionality

DORA includes proportionality provisions allowing smaller entities to apply a simplified framework. As a bank, however, you are expected to apply the full framework.
