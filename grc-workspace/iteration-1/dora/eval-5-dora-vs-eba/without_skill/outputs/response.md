# DORA vs. EBA ICT Guidelines: Key Differences for an EBA-Compliant Bank

## Overview

Being compliant with the EBA Guidelines on ICT and Security Risk Management (EBA/GL/2019/04) is a solid starting point for DORA compliance, but it does not mean you are fully DORA compliant. DORA introduces significantly more prescriptive and detailed requirements in several areas, and creates entirely new obligations that have no equivalent in the EBA Guidelines.

---

## Legal Nature: A Fundamental Difference

The first critical difference is the **legal status** of each framework:

- **EBA Guidelines (EBA/GL/2019/04)**: Soft law. These are guidelines under the comply-or-explain principle. Supervisors are encouraged to incorporate them into supervisory practice, but they are not directly binding EU law in the same way as a Regulation.

- **DORA**: A directly applicable EU Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2022/2554). It is binding law across all EU Member States without need for national implementation. Non-compliance exposes you to regulatory sanctions, not just supervisory criticism.

This difference means that your EBA Guidelines compliance may have been assessed on a principles-based basis, while DORA requires granular, article-by-article compliance with specific obligations.

---

## Area-by-Area Comparison

### ICT Risk Management Framework

**EBA Guidelines:** Required a comprehensive ICT risk framework at a principles level, covering governance, risk identification, protection, detection, response and recovery. Board involvement was recommended.

**DORA (Art. 5–16):** More prescriptive requirements including:
- Explicit board-level legal responsibility for ICT risk — not just a recommendation
- Board must formally approve ICT security policies
- More detailed requirements for ICT asset registers, specifically mapped to critical business functions
- Mandatory backup restore testing (not just backup policy)
- Mandatory, formally defined RTO/RPO targets for critical functions
- Detailed RTS specifying the precise elements the ICT risk framework must contain

**Likely gap:** Even with EBA Guidelines compliance, you may lack the formal board accountability structures, asset register depth, and documented RTO/RPO that DORA requires.

---

### ICT Incident Management and Reporting

This is one of the **most significant areas of new obligation** under DORA.

**EBA Guidelines:** Provided guidance on incident management processes and encouraged internal escalation and competent authority notification for major incidents, but without specific timelines or standardised reporting formats.

**DORA (Art. 17–23):** Creates entirely new, mandatory reporting obligations:
- Six specific classification criteria for determining whether an incident is "major"
- Quantitative materiality thresholds (set in implementing standards) that determine when reporting is mandatory
- **Three-stage mandatory reporting** to the competent authority with defined timelines
- Harmonised reporting templates for all EU financial entities
- Specific rules for payment-related incidents

**Likely gap:** You almost certainly do not have the multi-stage regulatory reporting process with defined timelines and standardised templates that DORA requires.

---

### Digital Operational Resilience Testing

**EBA Guidelines:** Recommended security testing including penetration testing and vulnerability assessments as part of good practice, but without specific requirements on frequency, types, or advanced testing.

**DORA (Art. 24–27):** Creates an entirely new mandatory testing framework:
- Mandatory annual basic testing programme covering specific test types
- TLPT (Threat-Led Penetration Testing) for larger, significant institutions — this is an intelligence-led, adversarial simulation on live production systems, conducted every 2–3 years
- Specific requirements on tester qualifications and independence
- Competent authority involvement in the TLPT process

**Likely gap:** You may have security testing in place, but not a formal DORA-structured annual programme, and almost certainly have not assessed TLPT applicability or conducted TLPT.

---

### ICT Third-Party Risk Management

**EBA Guidelines:** Required outsourcing and ICT third-party risk management, with provisions on contractual requirements. However, the EBA Outsourcing Guidelines (EBA/GL/2019/02, separate from the ICT Guidelines) were the primary framework for outsourcing.

**DORA (Art. 28–44):** Much more detailed and prescriptive:
- Mandatory Register of Information covering all ICT service arrangements (submitted to regulator annually)
- Nine specific mandatory contractual provisions for providers supporting critical functions
- Formal ICT concentration risk assessment requirements
- Mandatory exit strategies for critical arrangements
- EU-level oversight framework for systemically important ICT providers (cloud providers, etc.)

**Likely gap:** Your existing third-party contracts likely predate DORA and may lack required provisions on audit rights, data portability, and subcontracting controls. Your vendor registry is likely not in the format required for the Register of Information.

---

## Summary: Key Gaps After EBA Guidelines Compliance

| Gap Area | Priority |
|---------|---------|
| Multi-stage incident classification and reporting with timelines | High — entirely new obligation |
| Formal annual resilience testing programme (DORA test types) | High — no equivalent in EBA Guidelines |
| TLPT assessment and programme | High — entirely new obligation |
| Register of Information in DORA-required format | High — new obligation |
| Contract remediation for DORA-required provisions | High — likely significant work |
| Formal concentration risk assessment documentation | Medium |
| Exit strategies for critical ICT arrangements | Medium |
| Enhanced board-level ICT governance formalisation | Medium |

---

## Recommended Approach

Given your EBA Guidelines compliance as a baseline, we recommend:

1. Conduct a formal DORA gap analysis mapping your current state against each DORA article
2. Prioritise incident management and reporting — this has the most significant new obligations with no EBA equivalent
3. Assess TLPT applicability and engage your competent authority on TLPT scheduling
4. Audit all ICT service provider contracts against DORA's required contractual provisions
5. Build your Register of Information in the format required by DORA's implementing standards

Do not assume that EBA Guidelines compliance translates to DORA compliance. A systematic article-by-article gap assessment is the only reliable way to identify and address your specific compliance gaps.
