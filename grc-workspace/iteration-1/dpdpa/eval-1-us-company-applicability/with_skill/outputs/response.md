# DPDPA Applicability for US Technology Companies with Indian Customers

## Does the DPDPA Apply to Your Company?

**Yes — the DPDPA applies to your organisation.** India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDPA) has explicit extra-territorial reach under **Section 3**. The Act covers:

1. Processing of digital personal data **within India's territory**, AND
2. Processing of digital personal data **outside India** where it relates to offering goods or services to individuals **located in India** at the time of collection.

As a US company that has acquired Indian customers and processes their data (even on US servers), you are a **Data Fiduciary** under the DPDPA — the equivalent of what GDPR calls a "data controller." The Act's reach is clear: any organisation outside India that offers goods or services to individuals located in India is captured.

> **Key distinction:** The DPDPA applies only to **digital personal data** — data in digital form, or data originally in non-digital form that is subsequently digitised. Physical/paper records never digitised fall outside scope. This differs from GDPR, which covers all personal data regardless of medium.

**Full compliance deadline: 13 May 2027** (18 months from the Digital Personal Data Protection Rules, 2025 notified on 13 November 2025). Your organisation should be in active preparation now.

## Your Key Obligations as a Data Fiduciary

### 1. Lawful Basis for Processing (Section 4)

The DPDPA provides **only two lawful bases** — far narrower than GDPR's six:

| Basis | Provision |
|-------|-----------|
| **Consent** | Section 6 |
| **Certain Legitimate Uses** | Section 7 (8 enumerated categories — exhaustive list) |

There is **no general "legitimate interests" balancing test** under the DPDPA. If your current processing relies on GDPR legitimate interests (e.g., analytics, marketing, fraud prevention), you must map each activity to either consent or one of the 8 Section 7 categories, or obtain consent.

### 2. Notice Obligation (Section 5 + Rule 3)

Before or at the time of collecting personal data, you must provide a **standalone notice** to each Data Principal (individual). The notice must be:

- In clear, plain language (no legalese)
- **Independent** — not buried in terms and conditions
- Containing an itemised list of data collected
- Stating the specific purpose(s) of processing
- Identifying categories of recipients
- Stating the retention period
- Explaining how Data Principals can exercise their rights
- Explaining how to file a complaint with the **Data Protection Board of India (DPBI)**
- Explaining how to withdraw consent

### 3. Consent Requirements (Section 6)

Where consent is your lawful basis, it must be:
- **Free** — not bundled with service access
- **Specific** — tied to a particular purpose
- **Informed** — given after receiving the Section 5/Rule 3 notice
- **Unconditional** — no coercion
- **Unambiguous** — clear affirmative action (opt-in checkbox; no pre-ticked boxes)

Consent may be withdrawn at any time. The withdrawal mechanism must be as easy as giving consent.

### 4. General Data Fiduciary Obligations (Section 8)

| Obligation | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Processor contracts** | Appoint Data Processors only under written contracts specifying scope, purpose, security measures, and audit rights (Rule 16) |
| **Data quality** | Where data is used to make decisions affecting Data Principals, ensure it is accurate, complete, and consistent |
| **Security safeguards** | Implement appropriate technical and organisational measures per Rule 7 (encryption, access controls, MFA, audit logging) |
| **Data erasure** | Delete personal data when the specified purpose is fulfilled, consent is withdrawn, or erasure is requested |
| **Breach notification** | Report personal data breaches to the Data Protection Board **within 72 hours** (Rule 6) |

### 5. Data Principal Rights (Sections 11–14)

Your Indian customers (Data Principals) have the following rights, which you must be able to respond to:

- **Right to access information** (Section 11) — summary of data processed, identities of fiduciaries and processors
- **Right to correction, completion, updating, and erasure** (Section 12)
- **Right to grievance redressal** (Section 13) — you must provide a functional grievance mechanism; Data Principals must exhaust this before approaching the Board
- **Right to nominate** (Section 14) — to appoint someone to exercise rights in case of death or incapacity

### 6. Children's Data (Section 9)

If any of your Indian customers are under **18 years of age**, you must:
- Obtain **verifiable parental/guardian consent** before processing any data
- Implement age verification at registration (Rule 10)
- Prohibit tracking, behavioural monitoring, and targeted advertising directed at children

### 7. Cross-Border Data Transfers (Section 16)

Transfers of Indian personal data to US servers are currently **permitted**. The DPDPA uses a **blacklist approach** — transfers are allowed to all countries except those specifically restricted by Central Government notification. As of April 2026, no countries have been notified as restricted, so your US transfers are lawful. However, monitor the MeitY Official Gazette for future restrictions.

## Immediate Action Items

1. **Conduct a data mapping exercise** — identify all digital personal data collected from Indian customers and the purposes of processing
2. **Audit your lawful bases** — replace any "legitimate interests" reliance with consent or a valid Section 7 category
3. **Update privacy notices** — ensure standalone, Rule 3-compliant notices for Indian customers
4. **Review consent mechanisms** — remove pre-ticked boxes; implement affirmative opt-in
5. **Implement age verification** — address children's data requirements if your platform is accessible to under-18s in India
6. **Update vendor/processor contracts** — ensure Rule 16 compliance
7. **Establish a breach notification procedure** — 72-hour Board notification requirement
8. **Create a grievance mechanism** — mandatory for all Data Fiduciaries

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

| Violation | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| Failure to implement security safeguards | **₹250 crore (~USD 30M)** |
| Failure to notify breach within 72 hours | **₹200 crore** |
| Children's data violations | **₹200 crore** |
| Other violations | **₹50 crore** |

Full penalties apply from **13 May 2027**. The enforcement body is the **Data Protection Board of India (DPBI)**, with appeals to the Telecom Disputes Settlement and Appellate Tribunal (TDSAT).
