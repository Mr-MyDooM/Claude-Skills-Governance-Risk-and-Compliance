# India's DPDPA: Applicability to US Companies with Indian Customers

## Does the DPDPA Apply to Your Company?

**Yes, most likely.** India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA), passed in 2023, has extra-territorial scope similar to GDPR. If you are collecting and processing personal data from individuals located in India as part of offering goods or services to them, the DPDPA will generally apply to your organization, regardless of where you are headquartered.

The key trigger is whether you are:
- Collecting personal data from individuals in India, AND
- Offering products or services to those individuals

As a US company with Indian customers, you almost certainly fall within scope.

---

## Key Obligations as a Data Controller (Data Fiduciary)

Under the DPDPA, your company would be considered a **Data Fiduciary** (similar to "data controller" under GDPR). Here are your primary obligations:

### 1. Consent
The DPDPA requires obtaining **valid consent** from users before processing their personal data. Consent must be:
- Freely given
- Specific to the purpose
- Informed (users must know what data is collected and why)
- Unambiguous (clear affirmative action — no pre-ticked boxes)

Unlike GDPR, the DPDPA has a more limited set of legal bases for processing. There is no broad "legitimate interests" ground — consent is the primary basis for most commercial processing.

### 2. Notice Requirements
You must provide users with a clear **privacy notice** explaining:
- What personal data is collected
- The purpose of collection
- How data will be used and shared
- How users can exercise their rights

### 3. Data Principal Rights
Indian users (referred to as "Data Principals") have rights including:
- Right to access their data
- Right to correction of inaccurate data
- Right to erasure (deletion) of data
- Right to withdraw consent
- Right to grievance redressal

### 4. Security Measures
You must implement appropriate technical and organizational security measures to protect personal data from breaches, unauthorized access, and misuse.

### 5. Breach Notification
In case of a personal data breach, you will likely need to notify both the regulatory authority and affected users within a defined timeframe. The DPDPA requires prompt notification — generally within a short window (possibly 72 hours, similar to GDPR) to the Data Protection Board of India.

### 6. Data Localization and Cross-Border Transfers
The DPDPA has provisions around cross-border data transfers. Transfers of Indian personal data outside India may be restricted to certain approved countries, or require specific safeguards. Your legal team should review the current transfer rules and any approved country lists.

### 7. Children's Data
If any users are minors, special protections apply. Parental consent is required for processing children's data.

### 8. Vendor Contracts
If you use third-party processors (cloud providers, analytics vendors, etc.), you will need data processing agreements in place.

---

## Enforcement

The **Data Protection Board of India** is the supervisory authority responsible for enforcement. Penalties under the DPDPA can be substantial — reportedly up to several hundred crore rupees for serious violations.

---

## Recommended Next Steps

1. Conduct a data audit to identify all personal data collected from Indian users
2. Review your privacy notice and consent mechanisms for DPDPA compliance
3. Assess your cross-border transfer practices
4. Update vendor contracts to include data processing provisions
5. Establish a breach response procedure
6. Assign a point of contact for Indian data protection compliance
7. Consult with legal counsel familiar with Indian data protection law

Note: The DPDPA's implementing rules and specific requirements continue to evolve. Many operational details depend on subsidiary regulations and government notifications that are still being developed. Staying current with MeitY (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology) guidance is essential.
