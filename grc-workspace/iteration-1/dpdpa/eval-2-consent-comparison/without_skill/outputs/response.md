# DPDPA vs. GDPR Consent Requirements: A Comparison

## Overview

Both India's DPDPA (Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023) and the EU's GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) require valid consent for processing personal data, but there are several important differences your compliance team should understand.

---

## Consent Standards: Side-by-Side

| Element | GDPR | DPDPA |
|---------|------|-------|
| Freely given | Yes | Yes |
| Specific | Yes | Yes |
| Informed | Yes | Yes |
| Unambiguous | Yes | Yes |
| Clear affirmative action | Yes | Yes |
| Pre-ticked boxes | Not permitted | Not permitted |
| Withdrawal mechanism | Must be easy | Must be easy |

At a high level, both frameworks have broadly similar consent validity requirements. The devil is in the details and the surrounding legal architecture.

---

## Key Differences

### 1. Legal Bases for Processing

**GDPR** offers six lawful bases for processing:
1. Consent
2. Contract performance
3. Legal obligation
4. Vital interests
5. Public task
6. Legitimate interests

**DPDPA** is more restrictive. It primarily relies on consent as the main lawful basis for commercial processing. The DPDPA has "deemed consent" or legitimate use provisions, but these are narrower than GDPR's "legitimate interests" — they cover specific scenarios like government functions, employment, and emergency situations. Commercial organisations will generally need to rely on consent for most data processing.

**Implication:** Your team cannot use the broad "legitimate interests" basis under DPDPA the way you might under GDPR. More activities will require explicit consent.

### 2. Consent Withdrawal

Both frameworks require that withdrawal be "as easy as" giving consent. However, the DPDPA places somewhat more emphasis on this requirement, and the practical implementation (particularly for digital platforms) is expected to be closely scrutinised.

### 3. Consent for Children (Minors)

**GDPR:** Requires parental consent for children under **16** (though member states can lower this to **13**).

**DPDPA:** Requires parental/guardian consent for anyone under what is expected to be a higher age threshold — likely **18 years** under DPDPA provisions (India's standard age of majority). The exact threshold and verification requirements are still being implemented through regulations.

**Implication:** Your age-gating for India may need to be stricter than for the EU.

### 4. Notice Requirements

**GDPR:** Requires a privacy notice with specific elements (Art. 13/14), which can be embedded in a broader privacy policy.

**DPDPA:** Also requires a notice before or at collection, but the DPDPA emphasises that notice should be clear and accessible. The implementing rules are prescribing specific notice formats and elements. There is a stronger push for notices to be standalone and not buried in lengthy terms and conditions.

### 5. Bundled Consent

**GDPR:** While bundled consent (tying consent to service access) is disfavoured and in many cases invalid, it can sometimes be salvaged by relying on the "contract performance" basis for processing that is genuinely necessary.

**DPDPA:** Takes a similar approach but the limited legal bases mean there's less flexibility to rely on contract performance as an alternative. Bundled consent is likely to be treated as invalid more consistently.

### 6. Consent Records

Both frameworks require demonstrability of consent — you must be able to show that valid consent was obtained. This requires maintaining consent logs with timestamps, the version of the notice shown, and the specific purposes consented to.

---

## What Your Compliance Team Needs to Do

1. **Audit existing consent mechanisms** for India — check if they meet DPDPA's standards
2. **Review age verification** — you may need stricter age gates for Indian users than for EU users
3. **Evaluate reliance on legitimate interests** — many activities justified under GDPR legitimate interests may require consent under DPDPA
4. **Simplify privacy notices** — DPDPA expects notices to be accessible and plain-language
5. **Implement consent withdrawal** — ensure it's as easy as giving consent for Indian users
6. **Maintain consent records** — document consent in a way that can be demonstrated to regulators

---

## Bottom Line

The DPDPA consent requirements are broadly aligned with GDPR principles but with fewer flexible legal bases and a potentially stricter treatment of bundled consent. For compliance teams already operating a GDPR program, the primary adaptation is narrowing your reliance on non-consent legal bases and reviewing your age verification practices for India.

Note: The DPDPA's implementing rules and specific consent requirements continue to develop. Legal advice from Indian data protection counsel is recommended for detailed implementation questions.
