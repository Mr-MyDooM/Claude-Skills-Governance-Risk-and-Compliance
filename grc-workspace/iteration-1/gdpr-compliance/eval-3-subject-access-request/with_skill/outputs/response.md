# Responding to a Subject Access Request (SAR) Under GDPR

> **Legal Advice Disclaimer**: This guidance is informational and based on the GDPR text and established regulatory guidance (including ICO and EDPB guidance). It does not constitute legal advice. For high-stakes SAR disputes, enforcement risk, or complex multi-jurisdiction scenarios, consult a qualified data protection lawyer or your DPO.

---

## Legal Framework

A Subject Access Request (SAR) is made under **Article 15 GDPR** — the right of access. The right gives data subjects the ability to:
1. Confirm whether you process their personal data;
2. Obtain a copy of that data; and
3. Receive supplementary information about the processing.

The response deadline is governed by **Article 12(3) GDPR**.

---

## Part 1: What Your SAR Response Must Include (Art. 15)

### 1.1 Confirmation of Processing (Art. 15(1))
You must first confirm whether or not you process personal data relating to the individual. If yes, provide a copy of the personal data **and** all of the following information:

### 1.2 The Copy of Personal Data (Art. 15(3))
- Provide a **copy of all personal data** you hold relating to the data subject
- The copy must be in a **commonly used electronic format** if the request was made electronically (Art. 15(3))
- The **first copy is free of charge** (Art. 12(5)) — see fee rules below

### 1.3 Supplementary Information Required (Art. 15(1)(a–h))

Your response must also include all of the following:

| Element | Article |
|---------|---------|
| The **purposes** of the processing | Art. 15(1)(a) |
| The **categories** of personal data concerned | Art. 15(1)(b) |
| The **recipients** or categories of recipients to whom data has been/will be disclosed (including third countries) | Art. 15(1)(c) |
| The **retention period** or criteria used to determine it | Art. 15(1)(d) |
| Information about the data subject's **rights**: rectification (Art. 16), erasure (Art. 17), restriction (Art. 18), objection (Art. 21) | Art. 15(1)(e) |
| The right to **lodge a complaint** with a supervisory authority | Art. 15(1)(f) |
| Where data was **not collected from the data subject directly**: any available source information | Art. 15(1)(g) |
| The existence of **automated decision-making** including profiling (Art. 22(1)(4)), with meaningful information about the logic, significance, and envisaged consequences | Art. 15(1)(h) |

### 1.4 Cross-Border Transfer Information (Art. 15(2))
If personal data is transferred to a third country (e.g., US servers), inform the data subject of:
- The appropriate safeguards in place (Art. 46 SCCs, Art. 45 adequacy decision, etc.)

## Part 2: Deadline and Process (Art. 12)

### 2.1 Core Deadline — One Calendar Month (Art. 12(3))
- You must respond **within one calendar month** of receiving the request
- The clock starts from the **day after** you receive the request (not from identity verification, unless you request verification — see below)
- "One calendar month" means the same day of the following month, not 30 days

### 2.2 Extension — Up to Two Additional Months (Art. 12(3))
You may extend by a further two months (total: three months) if the request is:
- Complex, or
- You have received a number of requests from the same individual

**Requirements to use the extension**:
- Notify the data subject within the **first calendar month** of receiving the request
- Inform them of the reasons for the delay (Art. 12(3))
- Failure to notify within one month even if using the extension is itself a violation

### 2.3 Identity Verification (Art. 12(6))
- You may request **additional information** to confirm the requester's identity where you have "reasonable doubts" about identity
- You must not routinely demand ID for every SAR — only where genuinely uncertain
- Requesting verification pauses the response clock **only if** you had reasonable doubts; if the request was made in an authenticated portal (e.g., logged-in account), you likely already know who they are
- Do not request disproportionate information; a utility bill is generally not needed if you can cross-reference account data

### 2.4 Free of Charge (Art. 12(5))
- Responses must be provided **free of charge**
- Exception: where requests are **manifestly unfounded or excessive**, you may:
  - Charge a reasonable fee (based on administrative cost), OR
  - Refuse to act (but must justify this decision)
- You bear the burden of demonstrating a request is manifestly unfounded or excessive

## Part 3: What You Can Redact or Withhold

### 3.1 Third-Party Data — Redact to Protect Others (Art. 15; Recital 63)
The right of access must not "adversely affect the rights and freedoms of others" (Art. 15(4); Recital 63). This is the most common legitimate ground for redaction:
- **Redact** personal data of third parties contained within the data subject's records where disclosure would be unfair to those third parties
- Example: an email chain contains both the requester's data and a colleague's personal comments — redact the colleague's identifying information, but provide the requester's own data
- **Do not use this as a blanket excuse** to withhold all third-party communications — you must strike a balance

### 3.2 Legally Privileged Information
- Communications protected by **legal professional privilege** may be withheld
- This is a narrow exemption — applies to genuine legal advice, not general business communications

### 3.3 Disproportionate Effort Exception (Art. 15(3) — for additional copies)
- If a requester asks for **additional copies** beyond the first, you may charge a reasonable fee based on administrative costs for subsequent copies

### 3.4 National Law Exemptions (Art. 23)
Member States may restrict SAR rights for specific purposes (e.g., national security, crime prevention, protection of judicial independence). These are narrow and jurisdiction-specific:
- **UK GDPR / DPA 2018, Schedule 2**: Exemptions include crime/tax investigations, legal proceedings, regulatory activities, management forecasts, negotiations
- If you are a US company relying on Member State exemptions, identify which Member State's law applies to your EU customer's jurisdiction

### 3.5 What You CANNOT Withhold
- You cannot withhold data simply because it is embarrassing, commercially sensitive to you, or because responding is burdensome
- You cannot withhold data on the basis that you disagree with the purpose of the request
- "Manifestly unfounded" is a **high bar** — EDPB guidance confirms this applies only to requests made in bad faith or with no legitimate purpose

## Part 4: Consequences of Missing the 72-Hour / One-Month Deadline

*Note: The 72-hour rule applies to **data breach notification** (Art. 33), not SARs. The SAR deadline is one calendar month (Art. 12(3)).*

### 4.1 Regulatory Enforcement (Art. 77–83)
If you fail to respond to a SAR within the one-month deadline (or the extended three-month period where properly notified):

- **The data subject can lodge a complaint** with any EU supervisory authority (Art. 77(1)) — they do not need to be in the same country as you
- **The supervisory authority must investigate** the complaint (Art. 57(1)(f)) and has broad investigatory and corrective powers (Art. 58)
- **Administrative fines**: Failure to comply with a SAR is a violation of Art. 12 and Art. 15, which falls under **Art. 83(4)**: fines up to **€10 million or 2% of global annual turnover**, whichever is higher
- More serious/wilful violations may be escalated to **Art. 83(5)** tier: fines up to **€20 million or 4% of global annual turnover**

### 4.2 Enforcement Action
- Supervisory authorities can issue **reprimands, warnings, and corrective orders** (Art. 58(2))
- A supervisory authority can order you to comply with the request (Art. 58(2)(c)(d)(g))
- Orders from the supervisory authority are legally binding

### 4.3 Right to Judicial Remedy (Art. 79)
The data subject may also seek a **judicial remedy** against you, independently of the supervisory authority complaint. Courts in any EU Member State where you process data may have jurisdiction.

### 4.4 Reputational Risk
SAR failures are commonly publicised by supervisory authorities. UK ICO, CNIL, and DPA decisions are frequently published. This creates reputational risk beyond the fine itself.

## Part 5: Practical Response Procedure

### Step 1: Log and Acknowledge (Day 0–1)
- Record the request in your SAR register with the date received
- Acknowledge receipt (not legally required but best practice; it resets expectations)
- Assess whether identity verification is genuinely needed

### Step 2: Search All Systems (Day 1–20)
Search all systems, databases, backups, email servers, logs, and third-party processors where the individual's data may reside:
- CRM / customer database
- Email (search both sender and recipient)
- Support ticketing system
- Analytics / product logs
- HR systems (if employee)
- Backup systems (note: may be impractical to search — document why if not done)

### Step 3: Apply Redactions (Day 20–27)
- Review all responsive data
- Redact third-party personal data
- Redact legally privileged material
- Document every redaction with justification

### Step 4: Compile and Send Response (By Day 30)
- Cover letter explaining the response structure
- Copy of all personal data (redacted where justified)
- All Art. 15(1) supplementary information
- Method of delivery: secure, documented (e.g., encrypted email, secure portal)

### Step 5: Document
- Record what was searched, what was found, what was provided, what was withheld and why
- Retain documentation for accountability (Art. 5(2))
