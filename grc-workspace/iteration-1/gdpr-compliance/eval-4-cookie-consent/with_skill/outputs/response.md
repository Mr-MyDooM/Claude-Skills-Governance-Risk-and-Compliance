# Cookie Consent Requirements for Analytics and Marketing Pixels — EU Users

> **Legal Advice Disclaimer**: This guidance is informational and based on the GDPR text, the ePrivacy Directive, and EDPB/supervisory authority guidance. It does not constitute legal advice. Cookie compliance is an active enforcement area across EU member states; consult a qualified data protection lawyer or DPO before finalising your consent implementation.

---

## Short Answer

For **analytics cookies** and **marketing pixels** targeting EU users, **consent is required** under both the ePrivacy Directive (ePD) and the GDPR. **Legitimate interests cannot be relied upon** as a substitute for consent for analytics cookies or marketing pixels — this position is confirmed by the EDPB and major supervisory authorities including the CNIL, ICO, and DSK (Germany). Read on for the full analysis.

---

## Part 1: The Legal Framework — Two Overlapping Regimes

### 1.1 ePrivacy Directive (2002/58/EC, as amended 2009)
The ePrivacy Directive ("Cookie Law") governs the **placement of cookies and similar tracking technologies** on user devices. It applies **in addition to** the GDPR.

Key rule: **Article 5(3) ePD** — Storing or accessing information on a user's device (which includes cookies, pixels, local storage, fingerprinting) requires:
- The user's **informed consent**, UNLESS the cookie is **strictly necessary** for a service explicitly requested by the user

The ePD does not permit legitimate interests as an alternative to consent for non-essential cookies. **Consent under the ePD must meet GDPR consent standards** (Art. 2(f) ePD cross-references GDPR).

### 1.2 GDPR — Lawful Basis for Processing Personal Data Generated by Cookies
Cookies and tracking pixels typically collect personal data (IP addresses, device identifiers, browsing behaviour) — see **Art. 4(1) GDPR; Recital 30** (cookie identifiers explicitly named as personal data).

For the **processing of this personal data**, you also need a lawful basis under **Art. 6(1) GDPR**:
- For analytics tracking of individual behaviour: **Consent (Art. 6(1)(a))**
- For marketing pixels tracking and profiling individuals: **Consent (Art. 6(1)(a))**
- For truly aggregated, non-identifiable analytics: GDPR does not apply (Recital 26)

## Part 2: Consent Requirements — What Valid Consent Looks Like

Consent must meet the requirements of **Article 7 GDPR** and **Recital 32**:

### 2.1 The Four Conditions (Art. 4(11); Art. 7)
| Condition | Meaning | Practical Implication |
|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| **Freely given** | No detriment for refusing; no bundling with terms | Cannot gate service access on cookie consent |
| **Specific** | Separate consent for each distinct purpose | Analytics and marketing must be separate opt-ins |
| **Informed** | Clear information about what is tracked and by whom | Name specific third parties (Google, Meta, etc.) |
| **Unambiguous** | Clear affirmative action required | Pre-ticked boxes, scrolling, continued browsing are NOT valid consent |

### 2.2 Additional Requirements
- **Withdrawal must be as easy as giving consent** (Art. 7(3)): You must provide a "Manage Cookies" or equivalent mechanism — not buried in the footer
- **No consent walls** (EDPB Guidelines 05/2020): You cannot make access to the website conditional on accepting non-essential cookies unless you offer a genuine, equivalent alternative (e.g., a paid tier)
- **No dark patterns**: The EDPB's Guidelines on Dark Patterns (03/2022) prohibit misleading consent interfaces — e.g., using deceptive contrast (grey "Reject" button, highlighted "Accept"), confusing language, or hidden opt-outs
- **Granularity**: Consent must be specific per purpose category — a single "Accept All Cookies" is only valid if users are also offered easy category-level control

### 2.3 Consent Banner Requirements
Your cookie banner must:
- [ ] Appear before any non-essential cookies are set (pre-consent requirement)
- [ ] Present "Accept" and "Reject" options with equal prominence (EDPB; CNIL guidance; Belgian DPA ruling)
- [ ] Not pre-tick any non-essential cookie categories
- [ ] Identify each cookie category and purpose clearly
- [ ] Name third-party processors (e.g., "Google Analytics," "Meta Pixel") — CNIL enforcement has required this
- [ ] Provide a link to your full Cookie Policy
- [ ] Allow granular choice (analytics vs. marketing as separate toggles)
- [ ] Record consent with timestamp and version (Art. 7(1) — you must be able to demonstrate consent)

### 2.4 Reconsent
- Consent should be renewed periodically (EDPB guidance suggests 12–13 months is a common supervisory authority expectation)
- Reconsent is required whenever the purposes or cookie list materially changes

## Part 3: Can You Use Legitimate Interests Instead of Consent?

### 3.1 The Short Answer: No — Not for Analytics or Marketing Pixels

**Legitimate interests (Art. 6(1)(f)) cannot be used as an alternative to consent for analytics cookies or marketing pixels.** This is the established position of:
- **EDPB Opinion 5/2019** and Guidelines on Consent
- **CNIL** (France): has issued fines against Google and Facebook for using legitimate interests for advertising cookies
- **ICO** (UK): "We do not consider analytics cookies can be regarded as strictly necessary" and legitimate interests does not apply to cookies under the ePD
- **DSK** (Germany Data Protection Conference): similar position
- **Belgian DPA** ruling in the IAB TCF case (February 2022): confirmed LI cannot substitute for consent under the ePD framework

**Why it fails the LIA (Legitimate Interests Assessment)**:
- **Purpose**: Website analytics is a legitimate purpose — passes the purpose test
- **Necessity**: Can you achieve the same purpose without individual-level tracking? Aggregate server-side analytics (e.g., server logs, privacy-preserving analytics tools) may achieve the same goal with less privacy impact — this undermines the necessity leg
- **Balancing**: Data subjects have a reasonable expectation NOT to be individually tracked across websites for advertising. The balancing test typically fails for marketing pixels, which are designed to track individuals across sites for commercial purposes

### 3.2 The Narrow LI Use Cases for Cookies
Legitimate interests is potentially available only for:
- **Security cookies**: Detecting fraud, bots, or intrusions — but these are typically strictly necessary anyway
- **Load balancing**: Technically necessary session management

The EDPB has consistently rejected LI as a basis for analytics or marketing tracking absent additional and compelling justification.

### 3.3 Alternatives to Individual-Level Analytics
Consider privacy-preserving analytics approaches that may reduce or eliminate consent requirements:
- **Server-side analytics**: IP-anonymised server logs without JavaScript tracking (may not require consent if truly anonymised)
- **Aggregate-only tools**: Plausible, Fathom, or Matomo configured without individual tracking
- **Consent-mode (Google Consent Mode v2)**: Collects modelled data where consent is refused — still requires a proper consent mechanism but reduces data loss from refusals

## Part 4: Marketing Pixels (e.g., Meta Pixel, Google Ads)

Marketing pixels are **higher risk** than analytics cookies for several reasons:
- They involve **cross-site tracking** of individuals across the web
- They enable **profiling** for targeted advertising (Art. 22 implications if fully automated)
- They transmit personal data to third-party controllers (Meta, Google) who become independent controllers of the data
- They have been the subject of multiple **major enforcement actions**: CNIL fines against Google (€150M, €90M) and Meta (€60M) for cookie consent failures; Irish DPC and others

**Requirements for marketing pixels**:
- [ ] Explicit, informed consent before any pixel fires
- [ ] Identify Meta, Google, etc. as recipients in your consent notice
- [ ] Inform users about cross-site tracking purposes
- [ ] Honour consent refusals — ensure pixels are technically blocked when consent is not given
- [ ] Assess whether profiling based on pixel data triggers **Art. 22** automated decision-making rights

## Part 5: Technical Implementation Requirements

### 5.1 Consent Management Platform (CMP)
Implement a GDPR-compliant CMP that:
- Fires NO non-essential cookies or pixels before consent is recorded
- Maintains a consent log with: user identifier, timestamp, consent version, choices made
- Provides easy access to withdraw/change consent at any time
- Does not load third-party scripts (analytics, pixels) until consent is confirmed

### 5.2 Demonstrate Consent (Art. 7(1))
You must be able to prove that consent was obtained:
- Store consent records server-side (not just in the user's browser, which they can clear)
- Retain consent logs for the duration of data processing plus limitation period

---

## Part 6: Enforcement Landscape

Cookie compliance is **actively enforced** across the EU:
- **CNIL (France)**: €150M against Google, €90M against Meta, €3M against Facebook Ireland (2022) for non-compliant cookie banners
- **Belgian DPA**: Major ruling against IAB Europe's TCF (Transparency and Consent Framework) in 2022, finding it violated GDPR
- **ICO (UK)**: Issued formal warnings and enforcement notices to multiple major websites
- **DSK (Germany)**: Coordinated cookie enforcement sweeps

Non-compliant cookie implementations face fines under **Art. 83(5) GDPR** (up to €20M or 4% global turnover for consent violations under Art. 7) and parallel enforcement under national ePD transposition laws.

## Summary

| Cookie Type | Lawful Basis Required | Legitimate Interests? | Action Required |
|-------------|----------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Strictly necessary | Not required | N/A | No consent banner needed for this category |
| Analytics (individual tracking) | Consent (Art. 6(1)(a) + Art. 5(3) ePD) | No | Opt-in consent banner |
| Analytics (truly anonymised) | None (outside GDPR scope) | N/A | Consider switching to privacy-preserving analytics |
| Marketing pixels | Consent (Art. 6(1)(a) + Art. 5(3) ePD) | No | Opt-in consent banner; name third parties |
| Profiling for advertising | Consent | No | Consent + Art. 22 assessment |
