# GDPR Data Breach Response: What to Do in the Next 72 Hours

## Situation Overview

You have a confirmed data breach: an employee accidentally emailed a spreadsheet containing names, email addresses, and account numbers of 500 customers to the wrong person. Under GDPR, this is a personal data breach — an unauthorised disclosure of personal data — and it triggers specific legal obligations.

Here is what you need to do, step by step.

---

## Is This a Notifiable Breach?

Yes, almost certainly. GDPR requires you to notify the relevant supervisory authority (data protection regulator) of a personal data breach unless it is "unlikely to result in a risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons."

This breach involves:
- **Account numbers**: financial information that could be used for fraud
- **Names and email addresses**: enabling targeted phishing attacks
- **500 individuals**: a significant number of affected people

The combination of financial data and contact information creates a genuine risk of harm to the affected individuals. You should treat this as a notifiable breach and proceed accordingly.

---

## The 72-Hour Clock Is Already Running

The 72-hour deadline to notify the supervisory authority starts from the moment your organisation became aware of the breach — not when a decision is made to report it. If an employee or manager knew about this and delayed escalating it, the clock may have been running for longer than you think.

**Act now.**

---

## What to Do in the Next 72 Hours

### Step 1: Contain the Breach (Do This Immediately)

- **Try to recall the email** if your email system supports message recall
- **Contact the recipient directly** — call them if you can. Explain the mistake and ask them to:
  - Delete the email and any attachments immediately
  - Confirm in writing (by reply email) that they have done so
  - Not forward or share the data
- **Secure the source**: identify where the spreadsheet lives, who has access to it, and whether it was sent anywhere else
- **Document everything**: record exactly when the breach occurred, when it was discovered, who discovered it, and what actions have been taken

### Step 2: Assemble Your Response Team (Hours 1–4)

- Notify your Data Protection Officer (DPO) if you have one
- Notify your legal counsel
- Notify senior management
- Open a formal incident record — you are required to maintain a written log of all breaches regardless of whether you report them externally

### Step 3: Assess the Risk (Hours 4–12)

Before you can complete your notification, you need to assess:
- Did the recipient open the email? (You may be able to check read receipts or ask them directly)
- Have you received confirmation of deletion?
- What is the realistic risk of harm to the 500 individuals?

**Risk factors that increase severity**:
- Account numbers are financial data — fraud risk is real
- Names + emails enable very convincing phishing attacks
- You don't know yet whether the recipient has seen or copied the data

**If you obtained confirmed deletion from a trusted recipient**: risk may be lower, but you should still notify the supervisory authority

**If you cannot confirm deletion**: assume the data has been exposed and treat as high risk

### Step 4: Notify the Supervisory Authority (Before 72 Hours)

You must file a breach notification with the relevant EU data protection authority. The authority depends on where your EU data subjects are located and/or where your EU Representative is based (if you are a US company without an EU office).

**What the notification must include**:
- A description of what happened — what data, how many people, how it happened
- The contact details of your DPO or privacy contact
- The likely consequences of the breach — e.g., risk of phishing or financial fraud
- What you have done or are doing to address the breach — recall attempts, deletion request, notification to affected individuals

**If you don't have all the information yet**: You can submit an initial notification with what you know and follow up with additional details later. It is better to notify on time with incomplete information than to miss the deadline while waiting for a perfect picture.

**Where to file**:
- Find the supervisory authority for the relevant EU country (each has an online portal or reporting form)
- Examples: ICO (UK), CNIL (France), DPC (Ireland), Datatilsynet (Denmark/Norway), etc.

### Step 5: Notify the Affected Individuals

In addition to the regulator, you must also notify the 500 affected customers if the breach is "likely to result in a high risk" to them. Given the nature of the data (financial account numbers + contact details), **you should notify them**.

**The notification to individuals should include**:
- A plain-language explanation of what happened
- What data was involved
- What you are doing about it
- What they should do to protect themselves:
  - Watch for suspicious account activity
  - Be alert to phishing emails that use their name and personal details
  - Contact you if they notice anything suspicious
- Your contact details if they have questions

**Send this as a direct email to each affected customer** — you have their email addresses, so a public announcement is not sufficient.

---

## What Happens If You Miss the 72-Hour Deadline?

Missing the deadline without good reason is itself a GDPR violation. The consequences include:

- **Fines**: The supervisory authority can impose significant administrative fines — up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover for breach notification failures, and up to €20 million or 4% of global turnover for more serious violations
- **Enforcement orders**: The authority can order you to take specific steps
- **Reputational damage**: Regulatory decisions are often published
- **Compensation claims**: Individuals who suffer financial harm because they were not warned in time may have grounds to claim compensation

In practice, regulators consider cooperation and self-reporting as mitigating factors. A company that promptly reports, cooperates fully, and takes concrete remedial steps is treated more favourably than one that delays or conceals a breach.

---

## After the 72 Hours — Follow-Up Actions

- **Complete your investigation**: Confirm whether deletion was achieved; document the outcome
- **Update your breach register**: Maintain a written record of the breach, your assessment, and all actions taken — this is required even for breaches you don't report externally
- **Root cause analysis**: Why did this happen? Auto-complete to the wrong address? No verification step for bulk customer data?
- **Remedial controls**: Consider implementing:
  - Email DLP (Data Loss Prevention) scanning for outbound messages containing personal data
  - Mandatory confirmation step before sending bulk customer data by email
  - Staff training refresher on data handling
  - Technical controls to prevent bulk customer data from being accessible via email at all
