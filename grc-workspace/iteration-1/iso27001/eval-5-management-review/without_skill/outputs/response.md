# Responding to a Major Nonconformity: "Insufficient Evidence of Management Review"

## Understanding the Finding

A major nonconformity (NC) is a serious audit finding that means you have failed to satisfy a mandatory requirement of ISO 27001. The management review requirement is one of the standard's core obligations — it exists because top management needs to be actively engaged in overseeing the information security management system, not just leaving it to the security team.

When auditors raise this NC, they typically found one of the following:
- No management review was conducted at all
- A review was conducted but not properly documented
- The documentation is too vague to demonstrate what was actually reviewed
- Only the security team attended, without genuine top management participation
- Not all required topics were covered

---

## What Management Review Actually Requires

ISO 27001 requires top management (the CEO, executive team, or equivalent) to formally review the ISMS at planned intervals — typically at least annually, and often twice a year.

The review must be a real meeting where executives actively engage with ISMS performance. It cannot be delegated entirely to the CISO or security team.

### Required Topics (Inputs)

The management review must cover:

1. **Follow-up from previous reviews** — Were last time's action items completed?
2. **Changes to context** — Has anything significant changed (regulatory environment, business strategy, new threats)?
3. **ISMS performance** — How well is the security programme working? This includes:
   - Results from internal and external audits
   - Security incidents and how they were handled
   - Security metrics and KPIs
   - Progress on security objectives
   - Corrective actions status
4. **Feedback from stakeholders** — Customer complaints, supplier issues, regulatory communications
5. **Risk assessment results** — Are risks being managed effectively?
6. **Opportunities for improvement** — What should we do better?

### Required Outputs

The review must produce documented decisions and actions, including:
- Specific improvement actions with owners and deadlines
- Any changes needed to the ISMS
- Resource decisions (budget, people, tools)

---

## What Evidence You Need

For each management review, you must have:

**Before the meeting:**
- A formal meeting invitation showing the review was planned
- An agenda that covers all required topics

**From the meeting:**
- Signed meeting minutes that are specific enough to show what was actually discussed
- An attendance list including top management (not just security staff)
- A list of decisions and action items, with owners and target dates

**Supporting evidence (referenced in or attached to minutes):**
- Security metrics/dashboard presented at the review
- Audit report summaries
- Risk assessment summary
- Status of security objectives

The minutes must be specific. "Security performance was reviewed" is not sufficient. "The Q3 vulnerability scan identified 3 high findings; all have been remediated as of [date]" is the kind of specificity auditors expect.

## Writing a Corrective Action Plan

A corrective action plan (CAP) must address both the immediate symptom and the underlying root cause. Here is how to structure it:

---

### CAP Structure

**Nonconformity Reference:** [Audit NC number]
**Clause:** ISO 27001:2022, Clause 9.3 — Management Review
**Classification:** Major
**Finding:** [Copy the exact wording from the audit report]
**Owner:** [Name and title]
**Target Closure Date:** [Within your certification body's deadline]

---

**1. Immediate Correction**

What you will do right away to fix the immediate problem:

- Schedule an emergency management review within [timeframe — typically 2–4 weeks]
- Prepare a comprehensive agenda covering all required inputs
- Conduct the review with top management in attendance
- Document detailed minutes covering all required topics and decisions
- Compile and retain all supporting evidence

*Evidence:* Meeting minutes, attendance list, action items log

---

**2. Root Cause Analysis**

Why did this happen?

Common root causes:
- No formal documented process for management review (no procedure, no agenda template, no schedule)
- Top management not aware that formal documentation and their participation was a mandatory requirement
- The security team was conducting internal security reviews but not escalating to a formal management-level review with proper documentation

---

**3. Corrective Actions**

Systemic changes to prevent this happening again:

| Action | Owner | Target Date |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| Create a Management Review Procedure documenting schedule, required agenda topics, documentation requirements | CISO | [Date] |
| Develop agenda and minutes templates covering all Clause 9.3 required inputs | CISO | [Date] |
| Schedule management reviews on executive calendar (minimum twice per year) | EA/COO | [Date] |
| Brief executive team on their responsibilities under ISO 27001 Clause 9.3 | CISO | [Date] |
| Conduct second management review per new procedure | CEO | [Date] |

---

**4. Verification**

How you'll confirm the actions worked:
- Conduct an internal audit specifically checking Clause 9.3 compliance before the closure audit
- Confirm two management reviews have been held with proper documentation
- Present evidence to the certification body at the closure meeting

---

## What to Submit to the Certification Body

When submitting your CAP and evidence:

1. **The completed CAP document** — showing root cause analysis and all planned actions
2. **Management review minutes** (the ones you conducted as immediate correction)
3. **Management Review Procedure** (your new documented process)
4. **Evidence of second review** (if time allows) or a confirmed scheduled date
5. **Internal audit results** confirming Clause 9.3 is now compliant

---

## Timeline

Most certification bodies give you **30–90 days** to close a major NC. A realistic timeline:

- **Week 1:** Conduct emergency management review with top management; document minutes
- **Week 1–2:** Write and approve Management Review Procedure and templates; brief executives
- **Week 3:** Internal audit to verify compliance
- **Week 4:** Submit CAP and evidence package to certification body

The key is acting quickly and demonstrating that you understand both what went wrong and how you've fixed the underlying process — not just the immediate documentation gap.
