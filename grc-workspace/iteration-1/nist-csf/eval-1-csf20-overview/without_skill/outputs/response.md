# NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 — Overview

## What Is NIST CSF 2.0?

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 is an updated version of NIST's widely-adopted voluntary cybersecurity framework, released in early 2024. It provides guidance to industry, government, and other organizations to manage cybersecurity risks. The framework is designed to be flexible, adaptable, and applicable across different sectors, sizes, and types of organizations.

---

## Key Changes from CSF 1.1 to CSF 2.0

### 1. Addition of the "Govern" Function
The most significant change in CSF 2.0 is the addition of a sixth core function: **Govern**. In CSF 1.1, there were only five functions. The Govern function elevates cybersecurity governance to the top level, recognizing that effective cybersecurity requires strong organizational leadership, policy, and accountability structures.

### 2. Broader Audience
CSF 1.1 was originally designed with a focus on critical infrastructure sectors. CSF 2.0 explicitly acknowledges that it is applicable to **all organizations** regardless of size, sector, or cybersecurity maturity level, including small businesses and non-profits.

### 3. Enhanced Supply Chain Risk Management
CSF 2.0 gives greater emphasis to cybersecurity supply chain risk management (C-SCRM), recognizing the increasing risks from third-party vendors and suppliers.

### 4. Improved Profile Guidance
The concept of organizational profiles (Current Profile and Target Profile) has been strengthened with more practical templates and guidance to help organizations assess gaps and plan improvements.

### 5. New Quick Start Guides
NIST introduced Quick Start Guides for different audiences (small businesses, enterprises, government) to make the framework more accessible.

### 6. Online Reference Tool
Informative references (mappings to other standards like NIST SP 800-53, ISO 27001, CIS Controls) have been moved to an online reference tool that can be updated more frequently than the core document.

## The 6 Core Functions of CSF 2.0

### 1. Govern (GV) — New in CSF 2.0
**Purpose**: Establish and monitor the organization's cybersecurity risk management strategy, expectations, and policy.

This function addresses the organizational context, risk management strategy, roles and responsibilities, cybersecurity policy, and oversight. It provides the foundation from which all other functions operate.

**Examples**: Documenting risk tolerance, defining who owns cybersecurity decisions, establishing cybersecurity policies, managing supply chain cyber risks.

---

### 2. Identify (ID)
**Purpose**: Understand cybersecurity risks to systems, assets, data, and capabilities.

Organizations need to know what they have (assets), who and what poses risk to those assets, and how to prioritize their security investments.

**Examples**: Asset inventory, risk assessments, vulnerability identification, business environment analysis.

---

### 3. Protect (PR)
**Purpose**: Implement safeguards to limit the impact of potential cybersecurity incidents.

This function covers access controls, data protection, security training, and the technical measures used to prevent attacks or limit their damage.

**Examples**: Multi-factor authentication, encryption, security awareness training, patch management, data backup.

---

### 4. Detect (DE)
**Purpose**: Identify the occurrence of cybersecurity events.

Organizations must have the capability to detect when something goes wrong through monitoring, logging, and anomaly detection.

**Examples**: Security information and event management (SIEM) systems, intrusion detection systems, log monitoring, user behavior analytics.

---

### 5. Respond (RS)
**Purpose**: Take action regarding a detected cybersecurity incident.

When an incident occurs, organizations need defined processes to contain, analyze, and mitigate the event, as well as communicate with stakeholders.

**Examples**: Incident response plans, communication procedures, forensic investigation, containment and eradication activities.

---

### 6. Recover (RC)
**Purpose**: Maintain plans for resilience and restore capabilities after a cybersecurity incident.

Recovery focuses on getting back to normal operations as quickly as possible while incorporating lessons learned into future improvements.

**Examples**: Disaster recovery plans, business continuity procedures, system restoration, post-incident reviews.

---

## How the Functions Work Together

The six functions are not sequential steps — they represent ongoing, parallel activities. Govern provides the strategic direction for all functions. Identify feeds into Protect. Detect feeds into Respond, which feeds into Recover. Lessons learned from incidents feed back into Govern and Identify to improve the overall program.

---

## Summary of Changes at a Glance

| Aspect | CSF 1.1 | CSF 2.0 |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Number of Functions | 5 | 6 (adds Govern) |
| Primary Audience | Critical Infrastructure | All organizations |
| Supply Chain Emphasis | Limited | Expanded |
| Profile Guidance | Basic | Enhanced with templates |
| Quick Start Guides | None | Available for multiple audiences |

---

## Why CSF 2.0 Matters

CSF 2.0 reflects the evolution of the threat landscape and organizational cybersecurity practices over the past decade. By elevating governance, expanding supply chain risk management, and broadening the intended audience, it provides a more complete and practical framework for organizations at any stage of their cybersecurity journey.
