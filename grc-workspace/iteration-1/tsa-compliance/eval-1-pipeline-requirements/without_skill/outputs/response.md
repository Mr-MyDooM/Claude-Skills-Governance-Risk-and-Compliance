# TSA Cybersecurity Requirements for Natural Gas Pipeline Operators

## Overview

The Transportation Security Administration (TSA) has issued a series of Security Directives for pipeline operators following the Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack in May 2021. If your natural gas pipeline is under TSA jurisdiction — typically because TSA has designated your facility as critical infrastructure — you are subject to these mandatory cybersecurity requirements.

---

## Background: Why TSA Issued Pipeline Security Directives

Before May 2021, TSA's pipeline cybersecurity requirements were largely voluntary guidelines. The Colonial Pipeline incident, which caused significant fuel supply disruption across the eastern United States, prompted TSA to issue emergency Security Directives with mandatory requirements. TSA has issued and revised these directives multiple times since then.

---

## Key Requirements Under TSA Pipeline Security Directives

### 1. Cybersecurity Incident Reporting
Pipeline operators must report cybersecurity incidents to CISA (the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency) within a specified timeframe — generally understood to be within 24 hours of identifying a significant incident. Incidents include things like ransomware attacks, unauthorised access to control systems, and denial of service affecting operations.

TSA and CISA both play roles here: TSA sets the requirement, CISA receives the reports and provides technical assistance.

### 2. Designation of a Cybersecurity Coordinator
You must designate an individual as your Cybersecurity Coordinator who is available around the clock to serve as the primary point of contact with TSA and CISA. A backup designee is also typically required so that 24/7 availability is maintained.

### 3. Review of Current Cybersecurity Practices
Covered operators must conduct a review of their existing cybersecurity practices to identify gaps relative to TSA requirements. This review forms the foundation for a compliance plan.

### 4. Cybersecurity Implementation Plan
TSA requires covered pipeline operators to develop and submit a plan describing how they will implement the required cybersecurity measures. This plan must be reviewed and approved by TSA. The plan typically covers:
- Network segmentation between IT and OT systems
- Access controls for critical systems
- Continuous monitoring capabilities
- Patch management processes

---

## Core Technical Requirements

TSA's pipeline security directives focus on protecting what are called "Critical Cyber Systems" — the operational technology (OT) and industrial control systems that control physical pipeline operations, as well as connected IT systems.

The four main technical areas are:

**Network Segmentation**: Separating your operational technology (OT) environment — SCADA systems, PLCs, control systems — from your corporate IT network. The goal is to prevent an attack on IT systems from spreading to operational systems that control the physical pipeline.

**Access Controls**: Implementing strong access controls including multi-factor authentication (MFA) for remote access to critical systems, limiting privileged access, and ensuring vendor/third-party access is properly controlled.

**Monitoring and Detection**: Implementing capabilities to detect cybersecurity threats and anomalies affecting your operational systems. This includes monitoring both IT and OT environments.

**Patch Management**: Keeping systems updated with security patches using a risk-based approach that accounts for the operational realities of OT environments (e.g., vendor approval requirements, limited maintenance windows).

---

## Incident Response Plan

Beyond the technical controls, TSA requires covered operators to have a documented Cybersecurity Incident Response Plan (CIRP or IRP). This plan outlines how your organisation will detect, respond to, and recover from cybersecurity incidents. The plan must be periodically tested.

---

## Compliance Programme

Covered operators are generally required to have an ongoing compliance programme that includes:
- Annual assessments of the effectiveness of cybersecurity measures
- Regular reviews of network architecture
- Submission of compliance documentation to TSA

---

## 2024 Rulemaking

TSA has proposed converting these Security Directives into permanent federal regulations through a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) published in late 2024. Until a final rule is published, the existing Security Directives remain the binding requirements.

---

## Next Steps

If you believe your pipeline is subject to TSA jurisdiction:
1. Contact TSA to confirm your designation status
2. Review the specific Security Directives applicable to your operations
3. Engage legal counsel familiar with transportation infrastructure regulations
4. Work with OT/ICS cybersecurity professionals to assess your current posture

The Security Directives are designated as Sensitive Security Information (SSI), so the full text is not publicly distributed, but TSA will provide the directives directly to covered entities.
