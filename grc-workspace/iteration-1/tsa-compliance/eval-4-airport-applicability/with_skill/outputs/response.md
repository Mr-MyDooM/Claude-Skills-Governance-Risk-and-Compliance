# TSA Cybersecurity Regulations and Small Regional Airports

## Short Answer

TSA cybersecurity requirements may apply to your airport, but applicability depends on whether TSA has specifically designated your airport as a covered entity — it is not automatic based on size or sector membership alone. Small regional airports are not automatically exempt, but the primary substantive directives are currently focused on larger, higher-risk aviation entities. Here is the full picture.

---

## How TSA Determines Coverage: Designation-Based, Not Automatic

This is one of the most important points about TSA cybersecurity directives: **TSA designates covered entities individually**. Not every operator in a sector is automatically subject to the mandatory requirements. Coverage depends on:

- Whether TSA has formally notified your airport that it is subject to a specific Security Directive
- Your airport's risk profile and strategic significance
- Your passenger volume, cargo volume, and role in the national aviation system
- Whether TSA has conducted an assessment and determined your operations present a higher cybersecurity risk

**Action required**: If you have not received a formal notification from TSA designating your airport as a covered entity under a specific Security Directive, you should contact TSA's aviation security programme to understand your status.

---

## Aviation Cybersecurity: A Different Directive Series

The pipeline, freight rail, and transit Security Directives described in the SD Pipeline-2021 and SD 1580/1582 series do **not** apply to airports. Aviation has its own Security Directive and Emergency Amendment series.

TSA has issued Security Directives and Emergency Amendments specifically for:
- **Airports** (airport operators)
- **Aircraft operators** (airlines and cargo carriers)

These aviation-specific directives address cybersecurity requirements including:
- Network segmentation and access controls
- Incident reporting to CISA
- Designation of a Cybersecurity Coordinator
- Cybersecurity implementation plans

The specific directive text is Sensitive Security Information (SSI), and TSA provides it directly to covered entities.

---

## Airport Categories Relevant to Security Classification

TSA classifies airports under a separate risk-based framework for physical security purposes. The primary classification system for aviation security uses **airport categories** based on enplanement volumes and security risk:

| Category | General Profile |
|----------|----------------|
| **Category X** | Large hub airports; highest security requirements |
| **Category I** | Large and medium hub airports |
| **Category II** | Smaller hub airports |
| **Category III** | Non-hub airports with scheduled commercial service |
| **Category IV** | Small non-hub airports; lowest volume commercial service |
| **General Aviation** | Non-commercial; private/corporate aviation |

Small regional airports typically fall into **Category III or Category IV**, depending on whether they have scheduled commercial passenger service and their annual enplanement numbers.

**Cybersecurity implication**: TSA's mandatory cybersecurity directives for aviation have generally been applied first to higher-category (Category X and Category I) airports that represent greater risk to the national aviation system. However, this does not mean lower-category airports are permanently exempt — TSA's risk-based approach can extend coverage to lower-category airports if risk warrants it, and the 2024 NPRM signals an intent to broaden mandatory requirements across sectors.

---

## The 2024 NPRM and Future Coverage

In November 2024, TSA published a **Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM)** to convert existing Security Directive requirements into permanent federal regulations under 49 CFR. Key points relevant to your situation:

- The NPRM primarily addressed pipelines, freight rail, and passenger rail/transit sectors for the CRMP requirements
- Bus-only operators (incident reporting only) were included for the first time
- Aviation was not the primary focus of this NPRM, but aviation cybersecurity requirements continue to evolve through the separate Security Directive and Emergency Amendment process
- The comment period closed February 5, 2025; the final rule has not yet been published

As regulations mature, it is reasonable to expect that mandatory cybersecurity requirements will expand to more aviation entities over time, including smaller airports.

## What Small Regional Airports Should Do Now

Even if you are not currently a designated covered entity, proactive steps are advisable:

### 1. Confirm Your Status With TSA
Contact TSA's Office of Security Policy and Industry Engagement to understand whether your airport is currently subject to any active Security Directives or Emergency Amendments related to cybersecurity.

### 2. Understand Your Airport's Critical Cyber Systems
Regardless of regulatory status, identify your systems whose compromise could affect operations — access control systems, airport operations systems, fuelling systems, baggage systems, and any systems connected to air traffic control or airline operations.

### 3. Implement Baseline Cybersecurity Practices
Even without a mandate, CISA's Cross-Sector Cybersecurity Performance Goals (CPGs) — referenced in TSA's 2024 NPRM — provide a practical baseline for critical infrastructure operators of all sizes. Key baseline practices include:
- MFA for remote access and privileged accounts
- Network segmentation between operational and corporate systems
- Incident response planning and regular testing
- Patch management for all systems

### 4. Establish a CISA Reporting Pathway
Even before any formal designation, understanding how to report cybersecurity incidents to CISA is good practice. CISA's 24/7 Operations Center is reachable at **1-888-282-0870** or **CISAgov@mail.dhs.gov**.

### 5. Monitor TSA Regulatory Developments
Follow TSA announcements and the final rule rulemaking process. If the rule is finalised and coverage is expanded, you will want lead time to implement required controls.

## Summary

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Are all airports automatically covered by TSA cybersecurity directives? | No — TSA designates covered entities individually |
| Which airports are most likely covered today? | Larger, higher-risk airports (Category X, Category I) |
| Which directive series applies to airports? | Aviation-specific TSA Security Directives/Emergency Amendments (separate from pipeline/rail series) |
| Where does a small regional airport typically fall? | Category III or Category IV |
| Is coverage likely to expand to smaller airports? | Yes, over time — the trend is toward broader coverage |
| What should a small regional airport do now? | Confirm status with TSA; implement baseline cybersecurity practices; monitor regulatory developments |
