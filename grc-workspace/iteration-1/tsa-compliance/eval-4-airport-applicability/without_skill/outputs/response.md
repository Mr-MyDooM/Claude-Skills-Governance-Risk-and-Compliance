# TSA Cybersecurity Regulations and Small Regional Airports

## Do TSA Cybersecurity Regulations Apply to Your Airport?

The short answer is: it depends on your airport's classification and whether TSA has specifically identified your airport as subject to its cybersecurity requirements. TSA's cybersecurity requirements are generally tiered by risk and size, with the most stringent mandatory requirements applying to larger, higher-risk airports.

---

## How TSA Regulates Airport Security

TSA regulates airport security under the Aviation and Transportation Security Act (ATSA) and through a series of Security Directives and Emergency Amendments issued to specific categories of regulated parties. TSA's security requirements — including cybersecurity requirements — are generally applied based on the airport's classification within the national aviation system.

### Airport Security Categories

TSA classifies commercial service airports into security categories based primarily on annual passenger enplanements and risk. The general classifications are:

- **Category X**: The largest and highest-risk airports in the U.S. (major hub airports like LAX, JFK, O'Hare). These face the most stringent security requirements.
- **Category I**: Large hub airports
- **Category II**: Medium hub airports
- **Category III**: Small hub airports with scheduled commercial service
- **Category IV**: Smallest commercial service airports

There is also a **general aviation** category for non-commercial airports.

As a small regional airport, you likely fall into **Category III or Category IV**, or possibly the general aviation category if you do not have scheduled commercial passenger service.

---

## Cybersecurity-Specific Requirements for Airports

TSA has issued cybersecurity-focused Security Directives for the aviation sector. These directives — which are separate from the pipeline and surface transportation directives that received significant attention following the 2021 Colonial Pipeline attack — address requirements such as:

- Designation of a Cybersecurity Coordinator
- Cybersecurity incident reporting to CISA
- Network access controls
- Development of a cybersecurity incident response plan

However, these requirements have generally been applied first to **higher-risk, higher-category airports**. Mandatory cybersecurity directives have been issued to the larger hub airports (Category X and Category I), as these represent the greatest risk to the national aviation system.

---

## Does This Apply to Small Regional Airports?

**If you are a Category III or Category IV airport**, your situation depends on:

1. **Whether TSA has specifically notified your airport** that it is subject to a particular Security Directive or Emergency Amendment. TSA issues these notifications directly to covered entities. If you have not received such a notification, you may not currently be under a mandatory cybersecurity directive.

2. **Whether you have scheduled commercial service**. Airports with scheduled commercial passenger service under TSA oversight have more security obligations than general aviation airports.

3. **Your specific risk profile**. TSA can designate airports for coverage based on risk factors beyond just size.

**Important**: The absence of a current mandate does not mean you are permanently exempt. TSA's cybersecurity requirements have been expanding, and the trend across transportation sectors is toward broader coverage.

---

## Practical Guidance for Small Regional Airports

Even if you are not currently subject to mandatory TSA cybersecurity directives, there are important reasons to implement baseline cybersecurity practices:

**CISA Voluntary Resources**: CISA provides free cybersecurity resources for critical infrastructure operators of all sizes, including vulnerability assessments and cybersecurity performance goals that provide a practical baseline.

**Proactive TSA Engagement**: Contact TSA's Transportation Security Administration's aviation security division to understand whether your airport has been designated as covered under any active Security Directives, and to stay informed about evolving requirements.

**Common Sense Baseline**: Regardless of regulatory requirements, airports should protect systems that could affect safety and operations — access control systems, perimeter security systems, communications systems, and any operational technology.

---

## Summary

Small regional airports (Category III, Category IV, or general aviation) are generally not currently subject to the same mandatory cybersecurity directives that apply to large hub airports. However:

- TSA designates covered entities individually, so you should confirm your status directly with TSA
- TSA cybersecurity requirements are expanding across the aviation sector
- Voluntary compliance with cybersecurity best practices is advisable regardless of mandatory requirements
- If you receive scheduled commercial service, you likely have more security obligations than a general aviation airport

Contact TSA directly to determine whether your airport is currently subject to any active cybersecurity Security Directives.
