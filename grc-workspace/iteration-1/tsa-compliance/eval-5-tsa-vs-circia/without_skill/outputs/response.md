# TSA Cybersecurity Requirements vs. CISA CIRCIA: A Comparison for Pipeline Operators

## Overview

TSA cybersecurity requirements and CISA's CIRCIA (Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act) are two distinct but related regulatory frameworks. Pipeline operators subject to TSA jurisdiction need to understand both, as they create overlapping — but not identical — obligations.

---

## TSA Cybersecurity Requirements

TSA's cybersecurity requirements for pipeline operators come from a series of Security Directives issued starting in 2021 (following the Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack). These directives are administered by the Transportation Security Administration, a component of the Department of Homeland Security.

**Key characteristics of TSA requirements**:
- Apply specifically to **pipeline operators** (and other transportation sectors like rail and transit) designated by TSA as critical infrastructure
- Require a **comprehensive cybersecurity programme** — not just incident reporting, but building and maintaining operational security controls
- Include requirements for network segmentation (IT/OT), access controls, continuous monitoring, patch management, and cybersecurity planning documents (Implementation Plan, Incident Response Plan, Architecture Reviews, Assessment Plans)
- Require reporting of cybersecurity incidents to CISA within a short timeframe (generally within 24 hours for significant incidents)
- Require designation of a Cybersecurity Coordinator who serves as the point of contact with TSA and CISA
- Are currently **in force** as mandatory requirements

**Who enforces them**: TSA issues the directives and oversees compliance. CISA receives incident reports and provides technical assistance.

---

## CIRCIA (Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act of 2022)

CIRCIA is a federal law signed in March 2022. It directed CISA to create regulations requiring critical infrastructure entities to report cybersecurity incidents and ransom payments to CISA. CISA published proposed regulations (an NPRM) in 2024, but as of the time of this writing, the final rule has not been published — CIRCIA's reporting requirements are **not yet in effect** as enforceable regulations.

**Key characteristics of CIRCIA**:
- Administered by **CISA** (not TSA)
- Applies across **all 16 critical infrastructure sectors** — much broader scope than TSA's sector-specific directives
- Primarily a **reporting requirement** — focused on reporting incidents and ransom payments; does not require building a comprehensive cybersecurity programme in the way TSA directives do
- Proposed reporting timeframes: approximately **72 hours** for covered cyber incidents; **24 hours** for ransom payments
- Would cover a large number of entities across sectors that may not currently be subject to sector-specific cybersecurity mandates

---

## Key Differences

| Aspect | TSA Security Directives | CIRCIA |
|--------|------------------------|--------|
| **Administering agency** | TSA | CISA |
| **Sectors covered** | Pipeline, rail, transit (transportation sectors) | All 16 critical infrastructure sectors |
| **Current status** | In force and mandatory | Proposed rule; not yet final |
| **Core focus** | Comprehensive cybersecurity programme | Incident and ransom payment reporting |
| **Incident reporting window** | ~24 hours | ~72 hours (proposed) |
| **Programme requirements** | Yes — full cybersecurity programme required | No — reporting focused |
| **Coverage basis** | TSA designation of specific entities | Sector + size criteria in final rule |

---

## Do Pipeline Operators Need to Comply With Both?

**Yes, pipeline operators subject to TSA jurisdiction will likely need to comply with both frameworks**, though the nature of the obligations differs:

**Right now**: TSA Security Directives are in force. If TSA has designated your pipeline as covered, you must comply with all current directive requirements — including incident reporting to CISA within the required timeframe, the cybersecurity coordinator requirement, and the full Cyber Risk Management Program requirements.

**When CIRCIA is finalised**: CIRCIA will create an additional, broader incident reporting obligation covering potentially more entities and with specific requirements around ransom payment reporting. However, if you are already reporting to CISA within 24 hours under TSA requirements, you will already satisfy CIRCIA's longer 72-hour window.

**The reporting obligations may overlap**: Both TSA directives and CIRCIA funnel incident reports to CISA. CISA has indicated it intends to work to reduce duplicative reporting burdens, but the mechanics of coordination between the frameworks are still being developed.

---

## Practical Implications

For pipeline operators, the key practical points are:

1. **TSA requirements are the immediate priority** — they are in force now and carry real enforcement consequences
2. **CIRCIA adds ransom payment reporting** as a new obligation once finalised — specifically requiring reporting to CISA within 24 hours of making any ransom payment, which has no direct equivalent in current TSA directives
3. **Build your incident response process to satisfy both** — if your team can report to CISA within 24 hours (as TSA requires), you will meet CIRCIA's 72-hour window when it takes effect
4. **Monitor CIRCIA rulemaking** — the final rule will determine exactly which pipeline entities are covered and what the precise requirements are

---

## Summary

TSA requirements and CIRCIA are complementary frameworks — TSA focuses on building a comprehensive cybersecurity programme for designated transportation infrastructure operators, while CIRCIA focuses on ensuring that significant cyber incidents across all critical infrastructure sectors are reported to CISA promptly. Pipeline operators under TSA jurisdiction should treat TSA directives as their primary compliance obligation today, while preparing for CIRCIA's incident and ransom payment reporting requirements once the final rule is published.
